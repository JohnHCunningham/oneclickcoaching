## STRUCTURAL FRAMEWORK (MANDATORY)
Follow this sequence exactly:

### 1. CORE PROBLEM (NAME IT PRECISELY)
- State the problem better than the reader could
- Use pattern recognition, not accusation
- Introduce a paradox or contradiction that reframes their experience

**Use:**
- Pattern interrupt opening line
- Short, declarative sentences
- Contrarian or reframing insight

### 2. AGITATION (WITHOUT HYPE)
- Show the consequences of the problem continuing
- Contrast intention vs outcome
- Highlight hidden costs and loss aversion
- Use storytelling or real-world scenarios

**Avoid:**
- Fear mongering
- Inflated claims
- Absolute statements

### 3. ACTION VS INACTION (CONTRAST)
Present two clear paths:
- The cost of doing nothing
- The advantage of taking the right action

Use contrast to make inaction feel expensive and action feel inevitable.

### 4. SOLUTION (BUILT, NOT ANNOUNCED)
- Introduce the solution as a natural consequence of the insight
- Explain why it works before what it is
- Position it as system-level, not tactic-level

**Key rule:** The reader should think "Of course this is the solution".

### 5. VISION (TRANSFORMATION)
- Show the before vs after
- Emphasize predictability, control, relief, clarity
- Use anaphora (repetition at the beginning of sentences)

### 6. CONCLUSION (ANCHOR IT)
- Echo the core message using epistrophe (repeated ending)
- Reinforce inevitability
- Close with calm confidence, not pressure

## RHETORICAL DEVICES (THE FULL TOOLKIT)

Use 4-6 devices per blog post. Not every device in every piece—choose what serves the message.

### 1. Tricolon (Rule of Three)
Three parallel phrases for rhythm and memorability.
> "Measure what matters. Reinforce before drift. Coach while the moment is alive."
> "They don't need more training. They need training that sticks. They need training that shows up under pressure."

**Use for:** Headlines, key takeaways, emotional peaks

### 2. Antithesis (Contrasting Ideas)
Place opposing ideas side by side for clarity and punch.
> "Training teaches knowledge. Coaching builds execution."
> "Hope is not a strategy. Habit is."
> "The goal isn't to train more. It's to forget less."

**Use for:** Problem/solution transitions, memorable one-liners

### 3. Asyndeton (Remove Conjunctions)
Drop "and" to create speed and urgency.
> "They skip the Pain Funnel, rush the contract, pitch before qualifying, hope for the best."
> "Faster feedback. Better retention. Higher close rates."

**Use for:** Lists that need momentum, building tension

### 4. Polyptoton (Same Word, Different Forms)
Repeat a root word in different forms for emphasis.
> "The trained don't always perform. The performers were trained differently."
> "You can't coach what you can't see. And you can't see what isn't coached."

**Use for:** Reinforcing key concepts, creating linguistic hooks

### 5. Analogy (Familiar Comparison)
Compare abstract concepts to familiar experiences.
> "A sales methodology without reinforcement is like a gym membership without showing up."
> "Reviewing calls two weeks later is like watching game film after the season ends."
> "Delayed feedback is coaching in the rearview mirror."

**Use for:** Making complex ideas concrete, building understanding

### 6. Hyperbole (Strategic Exaggeration)
Exaggerate for emphasis—but use sparingly in B2B.
> "You've invested a fortune in training. You're getting pennies back."
> "Every day without reinforcement, your training investment evaporates."

**Use for:** Emotional peaks only, never for data claims

### 7. Antimetabole (Reverse Word Order)
Flip a phrase to create a memorable chiasmus.
> "Don't practice until you get it right. Practice until you can't get it wrong."
> "It's not about working harder on your sales. It's about your sales working harder for you."
> "You don't rise to the level of your training. You fall to the level of your habits."

**Use for:** Conclusions, quotable moments, reframes

### 8. Metaphor (Implicit Comparison)
State something IS something else (not "like").
> "Your CRM is a graveyard of good intentions."
> "Pressure is the X-ray of sales training—it reveals what's really there."
> "Methodology is the skeleton. Habit is the muscle."

**Use for:** Making abstract concepts vivid, creating imagery

### 9. Anaphora (Repeat Beginnings)
Start consecutive phrases with the same words to build momentum.
> "Not because they didn't care.
> Not because the training was wrong.
> Not because they lacked skill.
> But because pressure reveals habit, not intention."

**Use for:** Building emotional momentum, emphasizing lists, vision sections

### 10. Epistrophe (Repeat Endings)
End consecutive phrases with the same words to anchor a message.
> "Sales performance doesn't change in hindsight.
> It changes in execution.
> It changes when it matters."

**Use for:** Conclusions, emotional anchoring, calls to action

---

### BONUS: Internal Rhyme / Rhythm
Create subtle sound patterns without obvious rhyming.
> "When behavior becomes habit, results become predictable."
> "Structure creates freedom. Process creates performance."

**Use for:** Sentences you want readers to remember

---

### Device Combinations That Work Well

**Tricolon + Anaphora:**
> "They knew the methodology. They believed in the methodology. They just couldn't execute the methodology under pressure."

**Antithesis + Antimetabole:**
> "Training fills the mind. Coaching changes the behavior. But it's not about what you learn—it's about what you do with what you learned."

**Metaphor + Tricolon:**
> "Your pipeline isn't leaking. It's hemorrhaging—from skipped steps, rushed calls, and abandoned process."
